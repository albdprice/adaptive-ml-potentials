# Methods

## Theoretical Framework

### Euler's Homogeneous Function Theorem and Interatomic Potentials

A scalar function $f(\mathbf{x})$ is termed homogeneous of degree $d$ if scaling the argument by a factor $s$ yields:

$$f(s\mathbf{x}) = s^d f(\mathbf{x})$$

By Euler's homogeneous function theorem, such functions satisfy the differential relation:

$$\mathbf{x} \cdot \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = d \cdot f(\mathbf{x})$$

For a radial potential $V(r)$, this simplifies to:

$$r \frac{dV}{dr} = d \cdot V(r)$$

or equivalently in terms of force $F = -dV/dr$:

$$r \cdot F(r) = -d \cdot V(r)$$

This relation holds exactly for power-law potentials such as the Coulomb potential ($V \propto r^{-1}$, $d=-1$) and dispersion interactions ($V \propto r^{-6}$, $d=-6$). For these homogeneous potentials, a plot of $r \cdot F$ versus $V$ (the "Virial plot") yields a straight line with slope $-d$.

### Non-Homogeneity of Realistic Potentials

Realistic interatomic potentials describing covalent bonding, such as the Morse potential:

$$V_{\text{Morse}}(r) = D_e \left(1 - e^{-a(r-r_e)}\right)^2$$

are not homogeneous in the Euler sense. No constant $d$ satisfies the scaling relation across all $r$. Consequently, the Virial plot for Morse-type potentials forms characteristic loops rather than straight lines, indicating a position-dependent effective homogeneity degree.

### Universal Binding Energy Relations (UBER)

Rose, Ferrante, and Smith demonstrated that binding energy curves for a wide variety of metallic and covalent systems collapse onto a universal functional form when expressed in scaled coordinates. The Rose equation of state is given by:

$$E(a^*) = -E_c (1 + a^*) e^{-a^*}$$

where the reduced coordinate is defined as:

$$a^* = \frac{r - r_e}{l}$$

Here, $E_c$ represents the cohesive (binding) energy at equilibrium, $r_e$ is the equilibrium interatomic distance, and $l$ is a characteristic length scale related to the curvature of the potential at equilibrium. For the original Rose formulation applied to metals, $l = \sqrt{E_c / 12\pi B \Omega}$ where $B$ is the bulk modulus and $\Omega$ is the atomic volume.

### Adaptive Parameter Learning Hypothesis

Drawing on the success of adaptive exchange mixing in density functional approximations (aPBE0), we hypothesize that machine learning models can more efficiently learn interatomic potentials by predicting the Rose parameters $(E_c, r_e, l)$ as functions of local atomic environment descriptors, rather than directly learning the energy surface $V(r)$.

The rationale is threefold:
1. **Dimensionality reduction**: Three scalar parameters encode the essential physics of the binding curve
2. **Bounded targets**: The parameters have physical bounds and vary smoothly with atomic environment
3. **Physics-informed extrapolation**: The Rose functional form guarantees correct asymptotic behavior (repulsion at short range, decay to zero at large distances) even when extrapolating beyond the training data

## Computational Methods

### Potential Energy Functions

We employ the Morse potential as a realistic model for covalent bonding:

$$V_{\text{Morse}}(r; D_e, a, r_e) = D_e \left(1 - e^{-a(r-r_e)}\right)^2$$

with parameters: well depth $D_e$ (eV), stiffness parameter $a$ (Å$^{-1}$), and equilibrium distance $r_e$ (Å).

For the adaptive approach, we use the Rose equation shifted to match Morse conventions (zero energy at equilibrium):

$$V_{\text{Rose}}(r; E_c, r_e, l) = E_c \left(1 - (1 + a^*) e^{-a^*}\right)$$

where $a^* = (r - r_e)/l$.

Rose parameters are obtained by nonlinear least-squares fitting to Morse curves using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm as implemented in SciPy's `curve_fit` function, with bounds $E_c \in [0.01, 20]$ eV, $r_e \in [0.5, 10]$ Å, and $l \in [0.01, 5]$ Å.

### Dataset Generation

Synthetic datasets were generated by sampling Morse potential parameters from physically motivated ranges:

**Training data**:
- $D_e = 0.5 + 2.0 \cdot d_1$ eV, with $d_1 \sim \mathcal{U}(0.5, 1.5)$
- $r_e = 1.5 + 0.5 \cdot d_2$ Å, with $d_2 \sim \mathcal{U}(0.5, 1.5)$
- $a = 1.5$ Å$^{-1}$ (fixed)

This yields training potentials with $D_e \in [1.5, 3.5]$ eV.

**Test data (extrapolation)**:
- $d_1 \sim \mathcal{U}(2.5, 4.0)$, yielding $D_e \in [5.5, 8.5]$ eV

For each parameter set, potential energy curves were computed on a grid of 50 points spanning $r \in [0.7 r_e, 3.0 r_e]$.

The descriptors $(d_1, d_2)$ serve as proxies for atomic environment features (analogous to SOAP or ACE descriptors in production MLIPs).

### Machine Learning Models

Two approaches were compared:

**Direct Learning**: A Ridge regression model maps descriptors directly to the discretized potential energy curve:
$$\mathbf{d} \rightarrow \{V(r_i)\}_{i=1}^{50}$$

**Adaptive Learning**: A Ridge regression model maps descriptors to Rose parameters, which are then transformed to energies via the physics equation:
$$\mathbf{d} \rightarrow (E_c, r_e, l) \rightarrow V_{\text{Rose}}(r)$$

Ridge regression with regularization parameter $\alpha = 1.0$ was employed for both approaches. Input features were standardized (zero mean, unit variance) prior to training.

### Evaluation Metrics

Model performance was assessed using:

1. **Coefficient of determination** ($R^2$):
$$R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_i (V_i^{\text{true}} - V_i^{\text{pred}})^2}{\sum_i (V_i^{\text{true}} - \bar{V}^{\text{true}})^2}$$

2. **Mean squared error** (MSE):
$$\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i (V_i^{\text{true}} - V_i^{\text{pred}})^2$$

3. **Relative improvement**:
$$\text{Improvement} = \frac{\text{MSE}_{\text{direct}} - \text{MSE}_{\text{adaptive}}}{\text{MSE}_{\text{direct}}} \times 100\%$$

### Experimental Protocols

**Experiment 1 - Data Efficiency**: Training set sizes of $N \in \{10, 20, 40, 60, 80, 100, 120, 160\}$ samples were used while keeping the test set fixed (50 samples in extrapolation regime).

**Experiment 2 - Interpolation vs Extrapolation**: A fixed training set ($N=100$, $d_1 \in [1.0, 2.5]$) was evaluated against:
- Interpolation test: $d_1 \in [1.3, 2.2]$
- Mild extrapolation: $d_1 \in [2.5, 3.5]$
- Strong extrapolation: $d_1 \in [3.5, 4.5]$

**Experiment 3 - Noise Robustness**: Gaussian noise with standard deviation $\sigma \in \{0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20\}$ (as fraction of mean $|V|$) was added to training energies. Test data remained noise-free.

**Experiment 4 - Parameter Interpretability**: The quality of Rose parameter predictions was assessed by computing $R^2$ scores for each parameter individually.
