Adaptive Homogeneity Constraints for Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials: Theoretical Viability, Data Requirements, and Resource Landscape
1. Introduction to Physics-Informed Machine Learning Potentials
The rapid ascent of machine learning (ML) in computational materials science has fundamentally altered the landscape of atomistic simulations. By interpolating high-dimensional potential energy surfaces (PES) with ab initio accuracy at a fraction of the computational cost, Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials (MLIPs) have enabled simulations of unprecedented scale and duration. However, a persistent challenge remains the robust extrapolation of these potentials to thermodynamic conditions or configurations far removed from the training data. Standard regression models, such as high-dimensional neural networks (HDNNs) or Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), act as universal approximators but typically lack inherent knowledge of the physical laws governing atomic interactions, specifically in the asymptotic limits of dissociation or extreme compression.
This report evaluates a specific, physics-informed strategy to mitigate these limitations: the integration of adaptive homogeneity constraints, particularly within the framework of Morse-type potentials. The central hypothesis posits that while interatomic interactions are not strictly homogeneous in the mathematical sense (as defined by Euler’s theorem), they exhibit an "effective homogeneity" or universal scaling behavior—often described by the Rose Equation of State or Universal Binding Energy Relations (UBER)—that can be exploited to constrain the ML hypothesis space. By treating the homogeneity degree or the scaling lengths as adaptive, environment-dependent variables rather than fixed constants, MLIPs can theoretically achieve superior transferability and data efficiency.
The following analysis dissects the theoretical viability of this approach, deriving the necessary conditions for its implementation in neural network architectures. It further delineates the specific data generation protocols required to train such models—converting static spectral constants (as typified in spectroscopic literature) into rich, gradient-domain training sets—and comprehensively maps the existing ecosystem of databases and software resources (NIST, ExoMol, Duo, LEVEL) that facilitate this development.

4. Machine Learning Implementation Strategies
To operationalize adaptive homogeneity, specific architectural choices must be made regarding singularity handling and gradient integration.
4.1 Singularities and Pole Removal
A critical viability concern is the handling of singularities. While the Rose/Morse forms are finite at   , the underlying physics (Coulombic terms in ionic systems or effective core potentials) often involve    singularities. Standard neural networks with sigmoidal or ReLU activation functions struggle to approximate functions with poles, often requiring massive data to "learn" the divergence.27
Recent advancements in Rational Neural Networks (RationalNets) utilize rational functions (ratios of polynomials) as activation functions. These networks have been shown to possess superior approximation power for functions with singularities or poles, achieving exponential convergence rates where standard networks achieve polynomial rates.28
In the context of Morse potentials, the "singularity" is removable. The reduced coordinate    effectively regularizes the input. However, if the ML model is trained to predict the parameters of the potential, care must be taken at the equilibrium point where derivatives might vanish or become indeterminate (   limits). Implementation of "removable singularity" logic—such as predicting the product    rather than    directly, or using specialized loss functions that damp weight updates near singularities—ensures numerical stability.30
